Where the River Meets History

Florence isn't just a town—it's where the blues were born, where a Frank Lloyd Wright masterpiece hides in plain sight, and where the Tennessee River has been shaping lives for thousands of years.

Chapter 1

The Singing River

Long before any European set foot here, the Chickasaw people called this place home. They named the Tennessee River "the Singing River" for the distinctive sound the water made flowing over the rocky shoals—a musical quality that would prove prophetic for the region's future.

The Chickasaw were skilled farmers, hunters, and traders who built a sophisticated society along these riverbanks. The Florence Indian Mound, standing 43 feet high, predates Columbus by about a thousand years. It's still here—a testament to the deep history that flows through this land.

Chapter 2

Seven Men, One Vision

On March 12, 1818, seven ambitious men formed the Cypress Land Company and founded a town they named Florence—after Florence, Italy. The name was suggested by Ferdinand Sannoner, an Italian surveyor who saw potential for a great cultural center in this riverside location.

The seven founders were Thomas Bibb (who would become Alabama's second governor), General John Coffee (Andrew Jackson's trusted military commander), James Jackson, John McKinley (future U.S. Supreme Court Justice), LeRoy Pope, John Childress, and Dabney Morris. These weren't just land speculators—they were men who would shape Alabama itself.

But Florence wasn't built on speculation alone. It was built on the shoals—the shallow, rocky stretches of river that forced boats to stop, unload, and portage around the rapids. That inconvenience became Florence's first gift: a reason for people to stay, to build, to make a life.

Born Here

W.C. Handy: Father of the Blues

On November 16, 1873, William Christopher Handy was born in a log cabin on the corner of College and Marengo Streets. His father was a minister who considered secular music sinful. His grandfather had been enslaved. Yet from this small beginning, Handy would transform American music forever.

As a young man, Handy heard work songs in the cotton fields, spirituals in Black churches, and the peculiar sounds of the Tennessee Valley—the "blue notes" that didn't fit European scales. He wrote them down. He formalized them. He published "Memphis Blues" in 1912 and "St. Louis Blues" in 1914. These songs didn't just become hits—they became the foundation of jazz, rock and roll, and modern American music.

The log cabin where Handy was born still stands. You can visit it. You can stand where the Father of the Blues first heard music.

Grew Up Here

Sam Phillips: The Man Who Discovered Elvis

Sam Phillips was born on a farm outside Florence in 1923. As a boy, he picked cotton alongside Black workers and absorbed their music—the field hollers, the blues, the raw emotion. He never forgot it.

In 1950, Phillips opened Sun Records in Memphis. Over the next decade, he would discover and record Elvis Presley, Johnny Cash, Jerry Lee Lewis, Carl Perkins, and Roy Orbison. He didn't just find talent—he found a sound. That sound came from Florence.

"If I could find a white man who had the Negro sound and the Negro feel," Phillips famously said, "I could make a billion dollars." He found Elvis. But the sound he was looking for? He'd been hearing it since childhood in the Shoals.

The Muscle Shoals Sound

In the late 1960s and 70s, this small Alabama town produced more hit singles per capita than anywhere else on Earth—including New York, LA, and Nashville.

FAME Studios: Where Aretha Found Her Soul

Rick Hall grew up dirt poor in rural Alabama. His mother left when he was a child. His first wife died in a car accident. His second wife left him too. But Hall had one thing: an ear for music and a relentless drive.

In 1959, Hall founded FAME Studios in a converted tobacco warehouse. Over the next two decades, he would produce hits for Aretha Franklin, Wilson Pickett, Etta James, and dozens more. When Aretha came to FAME in 1967 to record "I Never Loved a Man," she found her voice. The rest is history.

What made the Muscle Shoals sound? A group of white musicians who played Black music with authenticity and soul. The rhythm section—known as the Swampers—backed everyone from Aretha to the Rolling Stones. Lynyrd Skynyrd immortalized them in "Sweet Home Alabama."

Years of History

Timeline

Key moments in Florence's history

100-500 CE

Copena Culture Builds the Indian Mound

Woodland period peoples construct the 43-foot ceremonial mound—the tallest domiciliary mound in the Tennessee Valley. This sophisticated culture was part of the Hopewell tradition.

1000-1450 CE

Mississippian Period

The Tennessee Valley becomes part of the broader Mississippian culture, with trade networks stretching across the eastern United States.

1540

Hernando de Soto's Expedition

Spanish explorer de Soto and his army pass through the region, becoming the first Europeans to encounter the Chickasaw people of the Tennessee Valley.

1805

Chickasaw Treaty

The Chickasaw Nation signs the first of several treaties that would eventually cede their Alabama lands to the United States government.

1816

Chickasaw Cession

The Chickasaw cede lands that include present-day Florence. General Andrew Jackson negotiates the treaty that opens northern Alabama to American settlement.

March 12, 1818

Florence Founded

The Cypress Land Company establishes Florence. Ferdinand Sannoner, an Italian surveyor, names it after Florence, Italy. Seven founders: Bibb, Childress, Coffee, Jackson, McKinley, Morris, and Pope.

1820

Dred Scott in Florence

Peter Blow brings the enslaved child Dred Scott to Alabama. Scott would later sue for his freedom in a Supreme Court case that helped precipitate the Civil War.

1830

LaGrange College Established

Alabama charters the first state teacher's college in the South. Today it's the University of North Alabama, one of the oldest public universities in the region.

1862

Civil War Comes to Florence

Union forces occupy Florence. The town changes hands multiple times during the war. Pope's Tavern serves as a hospital for both sides.

1873

W.C. Handy Born

William Christopher Handy is born in a log cabin in Florence. His father and grandfather were Methodist ministers who disapproved of secular music.

1880

Helen Keller Born

Helen Keller is born at Ivy Green in nearby Tuscumbia. An illness at 19 months leaves her deaf and blind. She would become a world-renowned author and activist.

1912

"Memphis Blues" Published

W.C. Handy publishes "Memphis Blues," one of the first blues songs ever written. His "St. Louis Blues" (1914) would become the most recorded song of its era.

1918

Wilson Dam Construction Begins

The federal government begins construction on Wilson Dam to provide power for nitrate production during World War I. At peak, 18,000 workers labor on the project.

1923

Sam Phillips Born

Samuel Cornelius Phillips is born on a farm outside Florence. He would later found Sun Records and discover Elvis Presley, Johnny Cash, and Jerry Lee Lewis.

1924

Wilson Dam Completed

The dam is completed after 6 years—the largest hydroelectric facility in the world. It generates 663 megawatts of power and tames the treacherous Muscle Shoals.

1933

TVA Acquires Wilson Dam

The Tennessee Valley Authority takes control of Wilson Dam, beginning FDR's sweeping program to modernize the rural South through electrification and flood control.

1939

Rosenbaum House Built

Frank Lloyd Wright designs the only Wright building in Alabama for newlyweds Stanley and Mildred Rosenbaum. They live there for over 60 years.

1959

FAME Studios Opens

Rick Hall opens FAME Studios in Florence. The name stands for Florence Alabama Music Enterprises. Within a decade, it would be the world's hit-making capital.

1962

"You Better Move On"

Arthur Alexander records "You Better Move On" at FAME—one of the first songs to blend country, gospel, and R&B. The Beatles and Rolling Stones both cover it.

1967

Aretha Franklin at FAME

Aretha Franklin records "I Never Loved a Man (The Way I Love You)" at FAME Studios—the session that launches her reign as the Queen of Soul.

1969

The Swampers Go Independent

Barry Beckett, Roger Hawkins, Jimmy Johnson, and David Hood open Muscle Shoals Sound Studio at 3614 Jackson Highway in Sheffield—immortalized by Cher and Lynyrd Skynyrd.

2013

"Muscle Shoals" Documentary

The documentary film "Muscle Shoals" brings worldwide attention to the region's musical heritage, featuring Mick Jagger, Keith Richards, Aretha Franklin, and others.

2018

Florence Bicentennial

Florence celebrates 200 years of history, culture, and community with year-long events honoring its past while looking toward its future.
