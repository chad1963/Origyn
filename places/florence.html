Welcome to Florence

Nestled along the banks of the Tennessee River, Florence is the kind of place that doesn't announce itself—it reveals itself. To outsiders, it's just another dot on the map of northern Alabama. To those who know, it's where some of the most important American music was born, where architectural masterpieces hide in plain sight, and where the river has been whispering stories for centuries.

The Seven Founders & A Renaissance Vision

On March 12, 1818, seven ambitious men formed the Cypress Land Company and founded a town they named Florence—after Florence, Italy. The name was suggested by Ferdinand Sannoner, an Italian surveyor who saw potential for a great cultural center in this riverside location. The seven founders were Thomas Bibb, John Childress, General John Coffee, James Jackson, John McKinley, Dabney Morris, and LeRoy Pope—men who would shape not just this town, but Alabama itself.

General Andrew Jackson himself had visited the area during the Creek War of 1813-1814 and encouraged settlement. The land had been ceded by the Chickasaw Nation through the Treaty of 1816, opening this territory to American expansion. The founders laid out a grid of streets, designated land for public buildings, and began selling lots at public auction.

But Florence wasn't built on speculation alone. It was built on the shoals—the shallow, rocky stretches of river that forced boats to stop, unload, and portage around the rapids. That inconvenience became Florence's first gift: a reason for people to stay, to build, to make a life.

The Chickasaw: First Stewards of This Land

For centuries before European contact, the Chickasaw people called the Tennessee Valley home. They named the river "the Singing River" for the distinctive sound the water made flowing over the shoals—a musical quality that would prove prophetic for the region's future. The Chickasaw were skilled farmers, hunters, and traders, maintaining a sophisticated society along these riverbanks.

Through the Chickasaw Cession of 1816, the tribe ceded their lands in what is now northern Alabama. This followed earlier treaties in 1805 that had begun the process of land transfer. By the 1830s, the Indian Removal Act forced the Chickasaw westward along the Trail of Tears. But their legacy remains—in place names, in archaeological sites, and in the very character of a land they shaped for generations.

The Father of the Blues Was Born Here

On November 16, 1873, in a log cabin on the outskirts of Florence, William Christopher Handy came into the world. His father and grandfather were Methodist ministers who saw music as the devil's work. But young W.C. Handy heard something else in the spirituals, work songs, and folk melodies that filled the air around him.

He would go on to write "St. Louis Blues," "Memphis Blues," and dozens of other compositions that laid the foundation for jazz, rock and roll, and modern American music. The cabin where he was born still stands, preserved as a museum. But his real monument is everywhere—in every blues riff, every jazz improvisation, every rock song that traces its DNA back to Florence, Alabama.

"The blues come from the need to live, from want, from desire. The blue notes—the flattened thirds and sevenths—that's the sound of longing."

W.C. Handy

Frank Lloyd Wright's Hidden Masterpiece

In 1939, Stanley and Mildred Rosenbaum were newlyweds with a plot of land and a dream. They wrote a letter to the most famous architect in America—Frank Lloyd Wright—asking if he would design them a modest home. To their astonishment, he said yes.

The Rosenbaum House is the only Frank Lloyd Wright building in Alabama, and one of the purest examples of his Usonian vision: affordable, beautiful homes for middle-class Americans. The Rosenbaums lived there for over 60 years, raising their family in a space where every detail—from the built-in furniture to the way light enters through clerestory windows—was designed by Wright himself.

Today, it's open to the public. Walking through it feels like stepping into a philosophy: that beauty and function aren't luxuries, they're rights.

The Mound Builders Were Here First

Long before European settlers arrived, the Tennessee Valley was home to a sophisticated civilization. The Copena culture, part of the broader Hopewell tradition, built earthen mounds throughout the region—ceremonial structures that connected communities across what is now the eastern United States.

Florence's Indian Mound is the tallest domiciliary mound in the Tennessee Valley, standing 43 feet tall. Built between 100 and 500 CE, it predates the later Mississippian period by centuries. The Chickasaw and Cherokee nations maintained connections to this land for generations, and the mound still holds significance for Southeastern indigenous tribes today.

Dred Scott: A Connection to History

Few know that Dred Scott—the enslaved man whose Supreme Court case would help ignite the Civil War—spent part of his early life in Florence. Scott was born around 1799 and was owned by Peter Blow, a Virginia planter who moved his family and enslaved workers to Alabama in 1818, the same year Florence was founded.

The Blow family settled in the Florence area, where young Dred Scott spent his formative years before being sold in 1830. He would later sue for his freedom in a case that reached the Supreme Court in 1857. Chief Justice Roger Taney's ruling—that African Americans could not be citizens—helped precipitate the sectional crisis that led to war. Florence's connection to this pivotal figure reminds us how deeply local histories intertwine with national destiny.

Sam Phillips: The Man Who Invented Rock & Roll

On January 5, 1923, Samuel Cornelius Phillips was born on a farm outside Florence. Growing up during the Great Depression, young Sam worked in the cotton fields alongside African American laborers, absorbing the blues, spirituals, and work songs that would shape his musical vision. He later said that working in those fields taught him to hear music differently.

Phillips would go on to found Sun Records in Memphis, where he discovered and recorded Elvis Presley, Johnny Cash, Jerry Lee Lewis, Carl Perkins, and Roy Orbison. He is widely credited with bridging African American blues with white country music to create rock and roll. When asked about his mission, Phillips famously said he wanted to record music that told the truth about what life was like for ordinary people. That vision was born in Florence.

The Hit Recording Capital of the World

Rick Hall was born in rural Franklin County, Alabama in 1932—into poverty so deep that his family lived in a sharecropper's shack without electricity or running water. His mother abandoned the family when he was young; his father was an alcoholic. Hall taught himself music as an escape, and that determination drove him to build something extraordinary.

In 1959, Hall founded FAME Studios (Florence Alabama Music Enterprises) in a tobacco warehouse above a drugstore. The breakthrough came in 1962 when Arthur Alexander recorded "You Better Move On"—one of the first songs to blend country, gospel, and R&B into what would become soul music. The Beatles would later cover it. The Rolling Stones would cover it. The door had opened.

What followed was unprecedented: Aretha Franklin flew from New York to record her legendary sessions that produced "I Never Loved a Man (The Way I Love You)." Wilson Pickett cut "Mustang Sally" and "Land of 1000 Dances." Etta James recorded the definitive version of "Tell Mama." Percy Sledge created "When a Man Loves a Woman"—the first Southern soul song to reach #1 on the pop charts.

The secret was the rhythm section—Barry Beckett on keyboards, Roger Hawkins on drums, Jimmy Johnson on guitar, and David Hood on bass. These white musicians from small-town Alabama had an intuitive feel for Black music that astonished artists from New York, Detroit, and Los Angeles. They became known as "The Swampers," and their groove defined an era. In 1969, they left FAME to open their own Muscle Shoals Sound Studio at 3614 Jackson Highway in Sheffield, where the hits kept coming.

"There's something in the water down there that makes people play different."

Aretha Franklin

On recording at FAME Studios, Muscle Shoals

Why Florence Matters Now

In an age of sameness—where every downtown looks like every other downtown, where chain stores have replaced local businesses, where places have lost their stories—Florence holds on.

The Court Street district still has locally-owned shops. The Shoals Theatre, built in 1936, still shows movies. The Tennessee River still flows past, unhurried and eternal. And people still gather on spring evenings to watch the sunset from McFarland Park, just like they've done for generations.

Florence isn't a museum. It's not frozen in time. It's a living place that has figured out how to honor its past while building its future. That's rare. That's worth knowing about. That's worth wearing.

"Florence has always been small—never more than 40,000 people. But somehow, this little river town keeps producing things that matter."

Notable Landmarks

Places that tell the Florence story

Museum

W.C. Handy Birthplace

The log cabin where the "Father of the Blues" was born in 1873, preserved with original artifacts including his famous trumpet and piano.

Historic Home

Rosenbaum House

The only Frank Lloyd Wright building in Alabama. Built in 1939 for $12,000, the Rosenbaums lived here for 60 years.

Archaeological Site

Indian Mound & Museum

A 43-foot ceremonial mound built between 100-500 CE by the Copena culture. The tallest domiciliary mound in the Tennessee Valley.

Historic Site

Pope's Tavern Museum

Built circa 1811 as a stagecoach stop. Served as a hospital for both Union and Confederate soldiers during the Civil War.

Engineering Landmark

Wilson Dam

Completed in 1924 after 6 years of construction. At the time, it was the largest hydroelectric facility in the world with 21 generating units.

Landmark

Renaissance Tower

The tallest building between Birmingham and Nashville. A symbol of Florence's forward-looking spirit.

Music Heritage

FAME Studios

Founded by Rick Hall in 1959. Where Aretha Franklin, Wilson Pickett, and dozens of legends recorded the Muscle Shoals Sound.

Entertainment

Shoals Theatre

Built in 1936 as a movie palace. Restored and reopened in 2019, this Art Deco gem hosts films, concerts, and community events.

Education

University of North Alabama

Founded as LaGrange College in 1830—the first state-chartered teacher's college in the South. Home of the live lion mascots Leo III.

Timeline

Key moments in Florence's history

100-500 CE

Copena Culture Builds the Indian Mound

Woodland period peoples construct the 43-foot ceremonial mound—the tallest domiciliary mound in the Tennessee Valley. This sophisticated culture was part of the Hopewell tradition.

1000-1450 CE

Mississippian Period

The Tennessee Valley becomes part of the broader Mississippian culture, with trade networks stretching across the eastern United States.

1540

Hernando de Soto's Expedition

Spanish explorer de Soto and his army pass through the region, becoming the first Europeans to encounter the Chickasaw people of the Tennessee Valley.

1805

Chickasaw Treaty

The Chickasaw Nation signs the first of several treaties that would eventually cede their Alabama lands to the United States government.

1816

Chickasaw Cession

The Chickasaw cede lands that include present-day Florence. General Andrew Jackson negotiates the treaty that opens northern Alabama to American settlement.

March 12, 1818

Florence Founded

The Cypress Land Company establishes Florence. Ferdinand Sannoner, an Italian surveyor, names it after Florence, Italy. Seven founders: Bibb, Childress, Coffee, Jackson, McKinley, Morris, and Pope.

1820

Dred Scott in Florence

Peter Blow brings the enslaved child Dred Scott to Alabama. Scott would later sue for his freedom in a Supreme Court case that helped precipitate the Civil War.

1830

LaGrange College Established

Alabama charters the first state teacher's college in the South. Today it's the University of North Alabama, one of the oldest public universities in the region.

1862

Civil War Comes to Florence

Union forces occupy Florence. The town changes hands multiple times during the war. Pope's Tavern serves as a hospital for both sides.

1873

W.C. Handy Born

William Christopher Handy is born in a log cabin in Florence. His father and grandfather were Methodist ministers who disapproved of secular music.

1880

Helen Keller Born

Helen Keller is born at Ivy Green in nearby Tuscumbia. An illness at 19 months leaves her deaf and blind. She would become a world-renowned author and activist.

1912

"Memphis Blues" Published

W.C. Handy publishes "Memphis Blues," one of the first blues songs ever written. His "St. Louis Blues" (1914) would become the most recorded song of its era.

1918

Wilson Dam Construction Begins

The federal government begins construction on Wilson Dam to provide power for nitrate production during World War I. At peak, 18,000 workers labor on the project.

1923

Sam Phillips Born

Samuel Cornelius Phillips is born on a farm outside Florence. He would later found Sun Records and discover Elvis Presley, Johnny Cash, and Jerry Lee Lewis.

1924

Wilson Dam Completed

The dam is completed after 6 years—the largest hydroelectric facility in the world. It generates 663 megawatts of power and tames the treacherous Muscle Shoals.

1933

TVA Acquires Wilson Dam

The Tennessee Valley Authority takes control of Wilson Dam, beginning FDR's sweeping program to modernize the rural South through electrification and flood control.

1939

Rosenbaum House Built

Frank Lloyd Wright designs the only Wright building in Alabama for newlyweds Stanley and Mildred Rosenbaum. They live there for over 60 years.

1959

FAME Studios Opens

Rick Hall opens FAME Studios in Florence. The name stands for Florence Alabama Music Enterprises. Within a decade, it would be the world's hit-making capital.

1962

"You Better Move On"

Arthur Alexander records "You Better Move On" at FAME—one of the first songs to blend country, gospel, and R&B. The Beatles and Rolling Stones both cover it.

1967

Aretha Franklin at FAME

Aretha Franklin records "I Never Loved a Man (The Way I Love You)" at FAME Studios—the session that launches her reign as the Queen of Soul.

1969

The Swampers Go Independent

Barry Beckett, Roger Hawkins, Jimmy Johnson, and David Hood open Muscle Shoals Sound Studio at 3614 Jackson Highway in Sheffield—immortalized by Cher and Lynyrd Skynyrd.

2013

"Muscle Shoals" Documentary

The documentary film "Muscle Shoals" brings worldwide attention to the region's musical heritage, featuring Mick Jagger, Keith Richards, Aretha Franklin, and others.

2018

Florence Bicentennial

Florence celebrates 200 years of history, culture, and community with year-long events honoring its past while looking toward its future.
