Did You Know?

The Muscle Shoals Sound

In the late 1970s, Muscle Shoals produced more hit singles per capita than any other city in the world—including New York, Los Angeles, and Nashville. Artists like Aretha Franklin, The Rolling Stones, Bob Dylan, and Etta James came to this small Alabama town to find "the sound."

— University of North Alabama Archives

Local Legend

W.C. Handy

"Father of the Blues"

1873 – 1958

Born in a log cabin in Florence on November 16, 1873, William Christopher Handy transformed the folk music he heard in the Tennessee Valley into the blues—a sound that would give birth to jazz, rock and roll, and modern American music. His compositions "Memphis Blues" and "St. Louis Blues" remain among the most recorded songs in history.

Cultural Icons

Stories That Shaped America

The Shoals region has been home to transformative figures whose legacies continue to inspire the world.

Helen Keller

1880 – 1968 • Tuscumbia

Born at Ivy Green in Tuscumbia, Helen Keller overcame deaf-blindness to become an author, activist, and lecturer. Her story with teacher Anne Sullivan remains one of history's most inspiring tales of perseverance.

The Swampers

Muscle Shoals Rhythm Section

The legendary session musicians who created the "Muscle Shoals Sound." They backed Aretha Franklin, The Rolling Stones, Bob Dylan, and Lynyrd Skynyrd immortalized them in "Sweet Home Alabama."

Rosenbaum House

Frank Lloyd Wright • 1939

The only Frank Lloyd Wright building in Alabama. Called "the purest example of Usonian architecture," this masterpiece was built with local red clay bricks and cypress wood.

Ancient Roots

The Singing River

For thousands of years before European contact, the Chickasaw people called the Tennessee River the "Singing River," believing that spirits in the water sang to them. The rocky shoals provided abundant mussels and shellfish, making this region a center of indigenous civilization.

The Florence Indian Mound, standing 43 feet high at the Port of Florence, predates Columbus by about a thousand years—a testament to the deep history that flows through this land.
